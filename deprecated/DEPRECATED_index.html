What went wrong?

The opioid crisis on a national scale can be traced in three major waves.

The first wave stems from early 1990’s efforts to reduce pain in patients. This stemmed from the argument that chronic and/or recurrent pain was a serious liability to productivity and quality of life, and the (now proven false) hypothesis that addiction to opioids was a rarity. Further fueling this were pharmaceutical companies advertising their prescriptions as nonaddictive. As a result, opioid prescriptions grew precipitously, and with them an increase in drug overdoses followed.

The second wave came as doctors and the government noted the uptick in prescription opioids were causing more drug overdose deaths, and as such began procedures to combat them, which came in two parts:

  1. First, pharmaceutical companies created a new formulation of the pill to extend the release window and resist to being crushed into powder.
  2. Secondly, doctors became more cautious with their prescriptions, due both to laws by the government and their own increasing knowledge about the addictiveness of such drugs.

Due to these efforts, prescription drugs alone stopped causing as many deaths. However, more and more people started turning to heroin to get the same effect, and thus the death toll due to heroin began to rise.

The third wave, supplementing the second wave, was the addition of synthetic opioids, such as fentanyl. These drugs were highly potent, and overdosing on them very easy. This is the most recent type of drug to be noted, and is notable for its sharp increase in recent years.

More statistics

  • Death rates from overdoses involving synthetic opioids increased in 21 states, with 10 states doubling their rates from 2015 to 2016. New Hampshire, West Virginia, and Massachusetts had the highest death rates from synthetic opioids. (1)
  • More than 46 people die every day from overdoses involving prescription opioids. (2)
  • From 1999 to 2016, more than 200,000 people died in the United States from overdoses related to prescription opioids. Overdose deaths involving prescription opioids were five times higher in 2016 than in 1999 (3)
  • After a steady increase in the overall national opioid prescribing rate starting in 2006, the total number of prescriptions dispensed peaked in 2012 at more than 255 million and a prescribing rate of 81.3 prescriptions per 100 persons. (4)
  • The CDC estimates the total economic burden of prescription opioid misuse in the US is $78.5 billion a year, including the costs of health care, lost productivity, addiction treatment, and criminal justice involvement. (5)

Closer to home: Massachusetts

Christie’s story is not a unique one. The opioid-related death rate in Massachusetts has surpassed the national average, with an especially sharp rise in the last two years.


The opioid crisis in Massachusetts has become particularly dire. In 2013–2014 alone, opioid-related deaths occurred in two-thirds of the cities and towns in Massachusetts.

What’s Being Done?

FILLER: buttons to show federal, state, local level initiative -->

On the federal level, the US Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) is focusing on:

  • Improving access to treatment and recovery services.
  • Promoting use of overdose-reversing drugs.
  • Strengthening our understanding of the epidemic through better public health surveillance.
  • Providing support for cutting-edge research on pain and addiction.
  • Advancing better practices for pain management.

The National Institutes of Health (NIH) - a component of HHS has recently (2017) met with pharmaceutical companies and academic research centers to explore:

  1. safe, effective, non-addictive strategies to manage chronic pain
  2. new, innovative medications and technologies to treat opioid use disorders.
  3. improved overdose prevention and reversal interventions to save lives and support recovery.

What's next?

There is still a lot to do. But we are working on it. Massachusetts is:

  1. Expanding OAT Treatment — opioid agonist treatment (OAT) can lower the ongoing risk for opioid-related death if it follows a non-fatal overdose. Developing a strategy to effectively deliver OAT could reduce the number of opioid-related deaths in the state.
  2. Considering Gender — Substance use trends differ depending on gender. This information can be used when developing prevention programs for both men and women.
  3. Developing Post-incarceration Treatment Plan — The short-term risk for opioid-related death is much higher for individuals who were recently released from prison.
